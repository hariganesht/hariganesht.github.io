The Bayesian Brain: Notes on Helmholtz, Bayes, and the Free Energy Principle

1. Introduction: perception as inference

Hermann von Helmholtz, writing in the 1860s, proposed that perception is a process of unconscious inference — the visual system does not passively record the world but actively constructs a hypothesis about what caused the sensory data. This was a prescient idea, and it sits at the heart of what we now call the Bayesian brain hypothesis.

The central claim is simple: the brain is, in some sense, a probabilistic inference machine. Rather than mapping sensory inputs directly to percepts, it maintains a generative model of the world and uses incoming data to update beliefs about hidden causes. Perception, cognition, and action are all instances of inference under this model.

Note: This post is mostly expository — I'm working through ideas from Clark (2016), Friston (2010), and Rao & Ballard (1999) for my own understanding. I'll flag where I'm uncertain or where the story gets messy.

2. Bayes' theorem, briefly

Let $s$ denote a hidden state of the world (e.g., the identity of an object) and $x$ denote sensory observations. The brain's goal, on this view, is to compute the posterior distribution over $s$ given $x$:

$$ \underbrace{p(s \mid x)}_{\text{posterior}} = \frac{\underbrace{p(x \mid s)}_{\text{likelihood}} \; \underbrace{p(s)}_{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{p(x)}_{\text{evidence}}} \tag{1} $$

The denominator $p(x) = \int p(x \mid s)\, p(s)\, ds$ is the marginal likelihood or model evidence — the probability of the observations under the model, averaged over all possible hidden states. This quantity is generally intractable, which is why exact Bayesian inference is hard and why approximations (like variational inference) are necessary.

Definition — Posterior belief
The posterior $p(s \mid x)$ represents the brain's updated belief about the hidden state $s$ after observing data $x$. It balances the prior $p(s)$ (what the brain expected before seeing $x$) with the likelihood $p(x \mid s)$ (how well different values of $s$ explain the observation).

3. Generative models and the likelihood

A key concept is the generative model: a joint distribution over hidden states and observations, $p(s, x) = p(x \mid s)\, p(s)$. The likelihood $p(x \mid s)$ specifies how hidden states generate observations — it is the model's theory of how the world works.

In a hierarchical generative model, hidden states are organized into levels, with higher levels generating (or constraining) lower levels. For a $K$-level hierarchy:

$$ p(x, s^{(1)}, \ldots, s^{(K)}) = p(x \mid s^{(1)}) \prod_{k=1}^{K-1} p(s^{(k)} \mid s^{(k+1)}) \cdot p(s^{(K)}) \tag{2} $$

This factorization is important: it means that each level only needs to talk to its immediate neighbors in the hierarchy. Rao and Ballard (1999) showed that this kind of structure can be implemented in a biologically plausible neural architecture — one that looks a lot like the visual cortex.

4. Predictive coding

Predictive coding is an influential theory of how the brain might implement approximate Bayesian inference in a hierarchical generative model. The basic idea:

  • Higher cortical areas send predictions (top-down) about what lower areas should be seeing, based on the current beliefs about hidden causes.
  • Lower areas send back prediction errors (bottom-up) — the discrepancy between what was predicted and what was actually observed.
  • The system minimizes prediction errors across the hierarchy.

Formally, if $\mu^{(k)}$ is the mean of the approximate posterior at level $k$, and $g^{(k)}(\mu^{(k+1)})$ is the prediction from the level above, then the prediction error is:

$$ \varepsilon^{(k)} = \mu^{(k)} - g^{(k)}\!\left(\mu^{(k+1)}\right) \tag{3} $$

The system updates $\mu^{(k)}$ by gradient descent on the total prediction error — which, under a Gaussian generative model, is equivalent to Bayesian inference. The update rule is:

$$ \dot{\mu}^{(k)} = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{F}}{\partial \mu^{(k)}} = \underbrace{\Pi^{(k)} \varepsilon^{(k)}}_{\text{from below}} - \underbrace{\frac{\partial g^{(k)}}{\partial \mu^{(k)}}^\top \Pi^{(k-1)} \varepsilon^{(k-1)}}_{\text{from above}} \tag{4} $$

where $\Pi^{(k)}$ is the precision (inverse variance) at level $k$ and $\mathcal{F}$ is the variational free energy (see below). The precision terms are crucial: they determine how much weight the system gives to prediction errors vs. prior beliefs — effectively implementing attention by modulating the gain on prediction error signals.

5. The Free Energy Principle

Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle is a grand unification: it proposes that all brain processes — perception, action, learning, attention — can be understood as minimizing a single quantity called variational free energy $\mathcal{F}$.

Free energy is an upper bound on surprise (negative log evidence):

$$ \mathcal{F}[q, x] = \underbrace{\E_{q(s)}\!\left[\log \frac{q(s)}{p(s, x)}\right]}_{\text{variational free energy}} = \underbrace{\KL\!\left[q(s) \,\|\, p(s \mid x)\right]}_{\geq\, 0} + \underbrace{(-\log p(x))}_{\text{surprise}} \tag{5} $$

Since $\KL[q \| p] \geq 0$, we have $\mathcal{F} \geq -\log p(x)$ — free energy is always an upper bound on surprise. Minimizing $\mathcal{F}$ with respect to $q(s)$ pushes $q$ toward the true posterior $p(s \mid x)$, and simultaneously minimizes surprise. This recovers the ELBO from variational inference:

$$ -\mathcal{F} = \underbrace{\E_{q(s)}\!\left[\log p(x \mid s)\right]}_{\text{expected log-likelihood}} - \underbrace{\KL\!\left[q(s) \,\|\, p(s)\right]}_{\text{complexity cost}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}} \tag{6} $$

Minimizing free energy is therefore equivalent to maximizing the ELBO. The first term rewards the approximate posterior for explaining the data well; the second term penalizes it for deviating too far from the prior. This tradeoff between accuracy and complexity is a recurring theme in Bayesian model comparison.

"To exist is to resist surprise — or rather, to be the kind of system that acts to maintain its existence in a world it can only partially observe." — roughly paraphrasing Friston

6. Caveats and open questions

The Bayesian brain hypothesis is compelling but not uncontroversial. A few honest concerns:

  • Tractability. Real Bayesian inference over the kinds of structured, high-dimensional hypothesis spaces needed for human cognition is computationally intractable. The FEP says brains minimize free energy — but it doesn't fully specify the approximate inference algorithm. That leaves a lot of room.
  • Falsifiability. The FEP is sometimes criticized for being unfalsifiable — with the right choice of generative model and priors, almost any behavior can be explained post-hoc. This is not unique to FEP (any sufficiently flexible model has this problem), but it is worth keeping in mind.
  • Priors. Where do the priors come from? Evolutionary pressure, development, individual learning? The Bayesian framework is silent on this, and getting the priors right is often where the real scientific work happens.

Despite these caveats, the framework has been enormously productive. It gives a unified vocabulary for talking about perception, learning, attention, and action — and it makes quantitative, testable predictions when combined with specific generative models and behavioral tasks.
